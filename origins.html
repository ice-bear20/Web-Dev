How HTML Began

HTTP (Hyper Text Transfer Protocol) is the foundation of the WWW (World Wide Web) first developed in 1989 by Tim Berner’s-Lee, the inventor of the WWW. The reason why HTTP/HTTPS is so important to the WWW is that it ensures that your website has not been tampered with, which gives your website credibility, and gives users peace of mind. Common people tampering with user experiences are malicious intruders trying to exploit unprotected communications (such as a man in the middle attack) to trick the user to give up sensitive information, install malware, or insert their own resources (like a backdoor of a sort). Every part of a webpage that travels between the user's machine and your websites, such as images, cookies, and scripts, can be manipulated if not protected correctly. Another group of people that will try and intrude into your webpages are 3rd party companies who don’t have the necessary malicious intent, but they are hindering the user, such as ISPs that inject ads into pages (Basques, 2015). HTTP has evolved from a simple request-response protocol into a universally used a respected foundation of the modern web that protects users and websites every day. Over the years it has adapted to the explosion of the internet through increased performance, scalability, and reliability.

HTTP has been there since day one of the WWW, and it has since evolved as well. The very first version of HTTP didn’t initially have a version number, but it was later numbered for HTTP/0.9. This version was extremely simple, as it consisted of a simple line of GET, which was the only method at the time, followed by the path to the resource (i.e., GET /my-page.html). The response was just as simple, as it produced a text only webpage <html> text-only web page </html>

Why HTML Was Important

  • It made web documents easy to create and read.
  • It allowed pages to link to one another through hypertext.
  • It created a common standard for structuring information online.
